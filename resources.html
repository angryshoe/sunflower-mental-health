How to Get Help - Hotlines

The following information belongs to the National Drug Helpline. Please visit this link to get more information.

If you are in an emergency/crisis, do not call a hotline. Call 911.

Hotlines are open telephone lines set up for a specific purpose; for mental health hotlines, you may speak anonymously about any topic and get help from professionals.


Hotlines should be called if you or a loved one exhibits any of the following signs:

Confused or strange thinking

Changes in sleeping patterns, eating habits, or behavior

Difficulty controlling anger and emotions

Isolation from family and friends

Erratic energy levels

Seeing or hearing things that may not be there

Feelings of uncontrollable hopelessness or despair

Thoughts of ending your life

Using drugs or alcohol to deal with your problems

Mental Illnesses

According to MedlinePlus, mental disorders are 'conditions that affect your thinking, feeling, mood, or behavior.' About 1 in 5 adults, or 20%, experience mental illnesses in a given year. This same percentage of teens experience a mental health condition between the ages of 13-18. The prevalence of mental illness in our society has only emphasized the need for mental health awareness. Hover below the following images to see the dangerous mental illnesses affecting teens and adults today.

Anxiety

The following information belongs to the National Alliance on Mental Illness. Please visit this link to get more information.


Anxiety disorder occurs when intense feelings of distress or nervousness begin to interfere with normal everyday activites. The following a few types of anxiety disorders:

General Anxiety Disorder: "Chronic, exaggerated worry" that interferes with concentration in common activites.

Social Anxiety Disorder: Irrational fear of embarrassment when it comes to social situations, generally leading to difficulty in conversations and isolation.

Panic Disorder: Characterized by instense feelings of terror that result in palpitations, chest pain, or shortness of breath.

Phobias: Characterized by severe reactions to irrational fears.

18.1% of the population suffers from anxiety in a given year


Treatment: If your anxiety begins to interfere with your everyday life, there are treatment options. In addition to psychotherapy, there are medication options, such as antidepressants and anti-anxiety medication. Another option to help relieve anxiety is getting exercise, trying meditation, and eating healthy. Click below to help destress and relieve anxiety on this website!

Depression

The following information belongs to the Americal Psychiatric Association. Please visit this link to get more information:

Depression occurs when one experiences intense feelings of sadness or lack of interest in once enjoyed activities. The following are some symptoms of depression.

1. Having a depressed mood, feeling worthless or guilty

2. Changes in appetite

3. Loss of energy, trouble sleeping

4. Difficulty thinking, thoughts of death/suicide


You are not alone: About 17% of the population will experience depression at least once in their life.


Treatment: The main treatments for depression are psychotherapy and medication.

Academic Pressure

Most people face stress daily. Most of the stress comes from school. The stress varies with what level of schooling students are in varying from elementary school to college.


Stress that Elementary School Students have

The clauses of stress are:

Being the Center of Attention : Eating an unusual looking lunch or being tardy
Presenting anything in front of the class : Being Scared that they will mess up and that will be passed around the entire school as gossip
Physical Education : Afraid of being the last one to be picked for any team sport or being required to meet the same exercise standard as the entire class

Symptoms:

Crying often

refusing to go to school

bedwetting


Stress that Middle School Students have

The clauses of stress are:

Homework load : Having more homework per night than the homework an elementary school student has
Busy Schedule : Having a lot of extracurriculars makes finding downtime be difficult
Financial Worries : Kids who cannot have the hottest brand of clothings or phones feel left out

Symptoms:

Retreating to bathroom

Self- harm

substance abuse


Stress that High School Students have

The clauses of stress are:

Parental Pressure: Kids are pressured to have good grades and trying to establish their independence at the same time
Earning good grades : Kids are struggling to please their parents of getting good grades and getting into a good school when getting into the college in the future as classes get more difficult
Social Acceptance: Kids who don't have their own group of friends worry about making friends and avoiding bullying

Symptoms:

Skipping meals or overeating

Sadness/depression

cutting classes


Stress that College Students have

The clauses of stress are:

Fear of Missing Out: Mistakes are made when balancing social activities and academic responsibilies at the samea time
Lack of Sleep: It is easy of kids to miss sleep since they have so much completing for their time
Missing Family and Friends: It is very common for kids to have homesickness and loneliness since they are living in the college dorm.

Symptoms:

Sleep problems

Social Isolation

Fluctuating weight


What Teachers can do:

Planning to make sure that students don't have a lot of homework night after night or that students don't have a lot of tests on the same day

Taking time to organize so students won't be overwhelmed

Listen to students' issues, writing them down on the board, so they can be addressed will help students put them aside


What Parents can do:

Having a lot of goal-related to activities is good for college, but don't overwhelm the kids

Make sure that kids have enough sleep. For kids ages 6-12, they should have 9-12 hours per night while 8-10 hours are recommanded for teens.

Be a good role model and model good self care for the kids to follow


All the above information comes from Community for Accredited Online Schools.

What is Eating Disorder?

Eating Disorder is a serious condition related to eating behaviors that negatively impact health, emotions and the ability to function in important areas of life.

Eating disorder often develop in teens and young adults years, but it can develop at other ages.

The main cause of eating disorder is focusing too much on weight, body shape and food.

Symptoms vary depending on the type of eating disorder.

×

Anorexia Nervosa

This eating disorder is characterized by abnormal low weight or intense fear of gaining weight. People with anorexia try to control their weight and body shape by extreme measure. For example, vomiting after eating and excessive exercise.

Bulimia Nervosa

People who have Bulimia have frequent episodes of binge eating and purging. They also limit eating during the day which will lead to more binge eating and purging. Basically, people who have this eating disorder will eat a lot in a short amount of time and try to get rid of the gain calories in an unhealthy way. People who have Bulima is probably preoccupied with their weight, shape, and also judged them harshly by their physical flaws.

Binge eating disorder

People who binge eat eat a lot. People who has this eating disorder eat quickly or eat a lot even if they are not hungry or being uncomfortabely full. After binge eating, they will not purge, but be ashamed of their eating habits. Being ashamed will lead to them eating in secret.

Rumination Disorder

The person who has Rumination persistently and repeatedly regugitate food after eating, but this eating disorder is not caused by anxoeria or bulimia. Sometimes food is rechewed, reswallowed, or spit out. Some people who has this eating disorder will spit out the food or eat less to avoid vomiting the food. However, this will lead to malnutrient. This is common in infancy.

Avoidant/Restrictive food intake Disorder

People who has this eating disorder fail to meet their minimum daily nutrient because of three reasons. First, they are not interested in eating. Second, they avoid food with certain characteristics such as color or smell. For the third and the last reason, they avoid eating for fear of choking or for other consequences of eating. This eating disorder's consequences are significant weight loss or failure to gain weight in childhood.

When to get help for yourself or a loved one

You should be getting help if you or a loved one is showing these symptoms:

Skipping meals or making excuses for not eating

eating much more food in a meal or snack than what is considered normal

Excessive exercise

expressing depression, disgust, shame or guilt about eating habit

eating in secret
